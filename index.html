How AI is Transforming Customer Support

2 min read

A few years ago, waiting two days for a reply to a support email was just normal. Today, customers expect answers in minutes, and AI is making that possible without burning out support teams.

The Old Problem

Most support teams spend the majority of their time on the same handful of questions. Password resets. Billing queries. "How do I do X?" tickets that get copy-pasted answers every single day. It is repetitive, slow, and leaves agents with less time for issues that actually need a human touch.

What AI Changes

AI-powered helpdesk tools are tackling this in a few concrete ways:

  • Auto-responses for common queries - The system recognises a familiar question and replies instantly, without an agent lifting a finger.
  • Ticket categorisation and routing - Instead of manually sorting every request, AI tags and assigns tickets based on topic, urgency, and customer history.
  • Suggested replies for agents - Even when a human needs to step in, AI drafts a response so the agent can review, tweak, and send in seconds.
  • Sentiment detection - AI flags tickets from frustrated customers so teams can prioritise before a small issue turns into a public complaint.

The Real Win

The goal is not to replace support agents. It is to remove the boring, repetitive parts of their day so they can focus on complex problems that actually need empathy and judgement. That is better for the team, and far better for the customer.

Platforms like Desk365 are a good example of this shift, combining ticketing, AI automation, and service management in a way that scales with a team rather than adding to their workload. As more SaaS companies build AI into their core workflows, the gap between fast support and great support is closing fast.

How I Use AI in Marketing

AI tools have become a core part of how I research, write, and plan content. Here is how I actually use them, not just the theory.

🤖 ChatGPT
My go-to for first drafts, brainstorming headline variations, and building content outlines. I use it to get from a blank page to a rough structure fast, then rewrite in my own voice.
⚡ Claude
Better for longer, more nuanced content like blog posts and landing page copy. Claude is less likely to sound robotic on the first pass and keeps tone consistent across long pieces.
🔍 Perplexity
I use this for research with sources. When I need to understand a topic fast, Perplexity pulls current, cited answers instead of guesses, which saves a lot of back-and-forth.
💼 GitHub Copilot
Useful when building basic content tools or automating repetitive tasks like formatting bulk content. Even without deep coding knowledge, it helps get small scripts done.
Real Example - Custom ChatGPT for Consistent Content

For a SaaS product blog, I set up a custom ChatGPT with a system prompt that locked in the brand voice, target audience, and a list of phrases to always avoid (like "leverage", "delve", "revolutionise"). Every time I needed a new post, I would start there instead of re-explaining the context. It kept the tone consistent across drafts and saved time on briefing.

Example system prompt: "You are a content writer for a B2B SaaS helpdesk tool. Our audience is IT managers and support leads at mid-size companies. Tone: direct, helpful, no fluff. Avoid: 'leverage', 'game-changer', 'revolutionise'. Always include a practical takeaway in the conclusion."
